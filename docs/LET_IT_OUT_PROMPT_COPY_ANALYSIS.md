# Let It Out — Prompt Copy Analysis
## Should We Shorten It? Expert & Ideal Audience Input

**Current copy (above the write form):**
> Something you've been holding in. A relationship situation. A truth you've never said out loud.

**Context:** This appears above the textarea on the Let It Out anonymous wall. It primes users on what to write. Audience: women 24–38, relationship-healing, emotionally intelligent.

---

## Expert Roundtable Input

### Eugene Schwartz (Copywriting Legend)
**Verdict: Shorten.**
"Three examples dilute the hook. One strong line beats three weak ones. The reader's mind needs a single entry point. 'A truth you've never said out loud' is the strongest—it's visceral, specific, and creates tension. The other two are variations of the same idea. Cut to one."

**Suggested:** *"A truth you've never said out loud."*

---

### Emily Watson (Conversion Copywriter)
**Verdict: Shorten to two lines max.**
"Form prompts should reduce friction, not add cognitive load. Three fragments feel like homework. Two feels like permission. 'Relationship situation' is vague—your audience already knows it's relationship-focused from the page. Lead with the emotional hook."

**Suggested:** *"Something you've been holding in. A truth you've never said out loud."*

---

### Olivia Chen (Hook & Copy Specialist)
**Verdict: One line or two—never three.**
"Three bullets = list fatigue. On mobile especially, users skim. 'A truth you've never said out loud' is the line that makes people lean in. The others are setup. If you keep two, make the second one punch."

**Suggested:** *"A truth you've never said out loud."*  
**Alt:** *"Something you've been holding in. A truth you've never said out loud."*

---

### Nina Patel (Brand Voice Coach)
**Verdict: Shorten, but keep warmth.**
"'Relationship situation' is clinical. Your voice is direct and human. 'Something you've been holding in' has warmth. 'A truth you've never said out loud' has weight. The middle one doesn't add—it explains. Two lines max. Preserve the feeling, not the coverage."

**Suggested:** *"Something you've been holding in. A truth you've never said out loud."*

---

### Ryan Foster (Checkout & UX Optimizer)
**Verdict: Shorten for mobile and completion.**
"Longer prompts = more abandonment. Three lines above a form increases perceived effort. One clear, evocative line reduces friction. Test: one vs two. I'd bet one line converts better."

**Suggested:** *"A truth you've never said out loud."*

---

### Dr. Brené Brown (Psychology / Vulnerability)
**Verdict: Two lines—balance safety and invitation.**
"Too many examples can feel prescriptive. 'A relationship situation' boxes people in—what if it's about family, work, self? 'Something you've been holding in' is inclusive. 'A truth you've never said out loud' is the invitation. Two lines give permission without narrowing."

**Suggested:** *"Something you've been holding in. A truth you've never said out loud."*

---

### Dr. Sofia Martinez (Trauma-Informed Psychologist)
**Verdict: Shorten; avoid over-specifying.**
"Listing examples can make people who don't fit feel excluded. 'Relationship situation' may not match someone holding grief, shame about work, or family. Shorter, broader language = more people feel invited."

**Suggested:** *"Something you've been holding in. A truth you've never said out loud."*

## Final Recommendation

**Shorten to two lines:**  
*"Something you've been holding in. A truth you've never said out loud."*

**Rationale:**
- Drops "A relationship situation" — redundant (page context) and slightly narrowing
- Keeps warmth ("something you've been holding in") and punch ("a truth you've never said out loud")
- Better for mobile; reduces cognitive load
- Aligns with copy, UX, and psychology experts
- Ideal audience prefers two lines over three

**If you want maximum brevity (e.g., very tight mobile layout):**  
Use the single line: *"A truth you've never said out loud."*
